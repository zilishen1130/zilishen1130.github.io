As a Marxist feminist, my research lies at the intersection of technology and gender, combining qualitative and computational methods. I'm currently studying how platform capitalism reshapes gender, intimacy, and labor, especially in China and East Asia, and the small, improvised tactics of resistance people work out inside these systems.

I was born in a county nestled in the mountains where Chongqing, Shaanxi, and Hubei meet. Its name carries the character wū (巫), meaning shaman or diviner, and that old, mist-soaked lineage of the southwest has never quite left me. I spent my early years there, among steep green hills and the waters of the Yangtze. Though I've now lived in cities longer than I lived among those hills, the memory of being a marginal person, an outsider, keeps reshaping me in different forms. It sharpens my interest in social stratification and mobility, and calls back, every so often, the misty rain of the Three Gorges I still hold in memory.

Most of my friends call me by the last character of my name, Lìlì (力力). In Chinese, 力 (lì) means power, a character seldom given to girls. It also sounds almost exactly like “Lily,” and there’s a roundabout little logic I’m fond of in that: the Chinese word for lily, bǎihé (百合), is also the word for love between women. So the flower found its way to me sideways, through two languages.
